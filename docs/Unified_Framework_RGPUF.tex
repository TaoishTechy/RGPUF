\section{The 1960s: Mainframe Genesis}\label{sec:1960s}

Before pixels, sprites, and GPUs, a handful of engineers and researchers turned mainframe computers and oscilloscopes into laboratories for simulated physics. \subsection{Foundational Titles}

The 12 games of the 1960s represent the birth of interactive physics simulation. \textbf{Tennis for Two} (1958) calculated the ballistic trajectory of a ball over a net on an oscilloscope, including gravity, bounce, and optional wind---the first video game with physics. \textbf{Spacewar!} (1962) on the PDP-1 introduced the first multi-body gravitational simulation, with two spaceships maneuvering in the gravity well of a central star using Newtonian momentum. \textbf{Space Travel} (1969) featured a scale model of the solar system with seamless 2D cosmos navigation, later used by Ken Thompson to develop Unix.

\textbf{Lunar Lander} (1969) presented the first turn-based physics puzzle requiring thrust level optimization against lunar gravity. \textbf{Hamurabi} (1968) introduced the first economic simulation with stochastic physics, using random number generation for variable crop yields and population dynamics. \textbf{Missile} (1969) was the first guided projectile arcade game implementing controllable propulsion and mid-flight correction. \textbf{Minskytron} (1961) demonstrated early chaos through feedback-driven patterns on a vector display.

\section{The 1970s: From Academic Demonstrations to Arcade Physics}\label{sec:1970s}

\subsection{Arcade Revolution Titles}
